The Mid-Market Rate vs. What Your Bank Gives You

The mid-market rate — also called the interbank or spot rate — is the midpoint between the buying and selling prices of any two currencies at any given moment. It's the rate you see on Google, on financial news sites, and on RateFlow. It's the rate that banks use when trading with each other.

It is almost certainly not the rate your bank offers you.

When you ask your bank to convert $1,000 USD to euros, they apply their own exchange rate, which includes a markup over the mid-market rate. This markup is rarely disclosed as a "fee" — it's simply baked into the number they quote you. A bank charging a 3% spread on a $1,000 transfer has effectively taken $30 from you without ever calling it a fee. On a $10,000 transfer, that's $300 gone.

SWIFT Fees and the Correspondent Bank Problem

On top of the exchange rate markup, traditional bank wire transfers typically involve additional charges that most customers never fully anticipate:

  • Sending bank wire fee: Usually $25–$45 per transfer at most US and European banks.
  • Correspondent bank fees: International wires often route through one or more intermediary banks. Each correspondent bank can deduct its own fee from the transfer amount — sometimes without prior disclosure. Your recipient may receive less than expected.
  • Receiving bank fee: The bank on the receiving end may charge an incoming wire fee of $10–$20.
  • Currency conversion fee (again): If the receiving bank converts the funds into local currency, they may apply their own exchange rate markup on top of everything.

Add it all up, and a $1,000 transfer via a traditional bank can easily cost $80–$120 in total — between 8% and 12% of the transfer value. For families sending remittances to developing countries, this is a significant and painful tax on money that's often desperately needed.

What a Modern Transfer Service Does Differently

Over the past decade, a new generation of money transfer services has emerged to fix exactly this problem. The best of them operate on a fundamentally different model. Rather than profiting from exchange rate markups, they convert at or very close to the mid-market rate and charge a small, transparent, upfront fee — typically 0.3% to 1.5% of the transfer amount, depending on the corridor and payment method.

On that same $1,000 transfer, the total cost with a quality service might be $5–$15. That's a difference of $65–$115 compared to a traditional bank — on a single transaction. For regular senders, this compounds into thousands of dollars saved per year.

What to Look for in a Transfer Service

Not all fintech transfer services are created equal. Here's what separates the genuinely good from the marketing-heavy mediocre:

  • Transparent fee disclosure: Before you confirm the transfer, you should be shown exactly how much the recipient will receive, in their currency, with all fees visible. Any service that makes this hard to find is hiding something.
  • Mid-market exchange rate: Check the rate they offer against the rate on RateFlow. A quality service should be within 0.5% of mid-market. More than 1% is a yellow flag; more than 2% is a red flag.
  • Transfer speed: Many modern services deliver same-day or next-day, compared to 3–5 business days for traditional bank wires. Speed matters when someone is waiting on funds.
  • Supported countries and corridors: Coverage varies. Make sure the service supports your specific sending and receiving countries before you commit.
  • Regulation and security: Use only FCA, FinCEN, or equivalent regulated services. Check for two-factor authentication and look for established track records.
