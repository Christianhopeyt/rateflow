XAF (CFA Franc): A Complete Currency Guide

✍️ RateFlow Team 📅 February 10, 2026 ⏱ 5 min read

The Central African CFA Franc — officially designated XAF by the International Organization for Standardization — is the currency used across six Central African nations: Cameroon, Chad, the Central African Republic, Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, and the Republic of Congo. With a combined population of over 60 million people and a significant diaspora spread across Europe, North America, and Asia, XAF is one of Africa's most economically significant currencies — and yet it remains poorly understood outside the region.

This guide covers everything you need to know: the history and structure of the XAF, its unique relationship with the euro, how to convert it to major currencies, and practical advice for travelers and diaspora members sending money to and from Central Africa.

A History Rooted in Colonial Economics

The CFA Franc was created in 1945, during the final years of French colonial rule, with "CFA" originally standing for Colonies Françaises d'Afrique (French Colonies of Africa). When these territories gained independence in the early 1960s, the currency was retained under a new arrangement with France, with the abbreviation reinterpreted as Coopération Financière en Afrique Centrale (Financial Cooperation in Central Africa) for the Central African group.

There are technically two CFA Franc zones in Africa. The West African CFA Franc (XOF) covers eight countries in West Africa, while the Central African CFA Franc (XAF) covers the six countries listed above. Despite having the same name and the same exchange rate against the euro, XAF and XOF are technically different currencies and are not interchangeable — though in practice they are accepted in some border regions.

The Euro Peg: Stability at a Price

The most defining characteristic of the XAF is its fixed exchange rate peg to the euro. Since 1999, when the euro replaced the French franc, 1 euro has always been worth exactly 655.957 XAF. Before 1999, it was pegged to the French franc at a fixed rate that had been in place since 1948.

This peg offers significant advantages. Because the rate never moves, businesses and individuals in XAF countries can plan imports, exports, and savings without worrying about currency fluctuation risk. Inflation tends to be lower and more predictable than in many other African currency zones. For the diaspora sending money home, the XAF/EUR rate is completely stable — which simplifies financial planning considerably.

However, the peg also has critics. Because XAF countries cannot devalue their currency to regain export competitiveness during economic downturns, they must instead adjust through painful internal measures like wage cuts or reduced public spending. Some economists argue the fixed rate keeps the CFA zone locked to European monetary policy in ways that don't always suit African economic conditions.

How to Convert XAF to USD and EUR

Converting XAF to EUR is straightforward because of the fixed peg. Divide any XAF amount by 655.957 to get euros. So 100,000 XAF equals approximately €152.45. Converting in the other direction, multiply your euro amount by 655.957.

Converting XAF to USD is slightly more complex because there's no direct peg — the XAF/USD rate changes constantly as the EUR/USD rate moves. To calculate it, you first convert XAF to EUR using the fixed rate, then convert EUR to USD using the current floating rate. For example, if 1 EUR = 1.08 USD, then 655.957 XAF = €1 = $1.08. Practical Tips for Travelers and Diaspora

  • For travelers arriving in Cameroon or other XAF countries: Avoid exchanging at the airport. Bureau de change rates in capital cities like Yaoundé and Douala are generally more competitive. Better yet, use a card that converts at the interbank rate with no foreign transaction fee.
  • For diaspora sending money home: Because the XAF/EUR rate is fixed, the most important variable is the service's fee and the EUR/USD (or EUR/GBP, etc.) rate they apply to convert your sending currency. Focus your comparison on those two factors.
  • Mobile money is transforming the landscape: MTN Mobile Money and Orange Money have significant penetration in Cameroon and across the region. Many international transfer services now support direct delivery to mobile money wallets, which is often faster and cheaper than bank-to-bank transfers in the region.
  • Always compare services: Corridors to Central Africa vary widely in cost between providers. What's cheap for sending to Cameroon may not be the best option for Gabon.
