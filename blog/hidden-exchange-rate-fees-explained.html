Every time you exchange currency or send money internationally, there are two possible costs: the fees you see and the fees you don't. The visible fee — a wire charge, a transaction fee — is easy to compare. The invisible fee — the exchange rate markup — is where most of the money disappears, and most people never notice it.

What Is the Mid-Market Rate?

The mid-market rate (also called the interbank rate or spot rate) is the midpoint between the global buying price and the global selling price of any currency pair at any moment. It's the rate you see on RateFlow, on Google, or on Reuters. It's what banks use when trading with each other.

It is almost never the rate a bank offers to a retail customer. That gap is the markup — and it's pure profit for the bank.

What Is a Rate Markup?

A markup is the percentage difference between the mid-market rate and the rate you are actually quoted. If the mid-market rate for USD/EUR is 0.9200 and your bank quotes you 0.8900, that's a markup of approximately 3.26%.

The markup is often called a "spread" in financial jargon. Unlike a flat fee, it scales with the amount you're transferring — so a 3% markup on $10,000 costs you $300, while the same markup on $100 costs only $3. This is why large transfers are where the hidden cost really hurts.

How to Calculate What You're Paying

Here's a simple formula to find your effective markup:

Markup % = ((Mid-Market Rate − Your Quoted Rate) ÷ Mid-Market Rate) × 100

For example: mid-market USD/XAF is 610.00. Your bank quotes you 590.00.

Markup = ((610 − 590) ÷ 610) × 100 = 3.28%

How to Avoid Hidden Fees

  • Always check the mid-market rate first on RateFlow before any exchange. This gives you the baseline.
  • Use a service that offers the mid-market rate — Wise is the most transparent for this.
  • Never exchange at airports or hotels — their markups are consistently the worst.
  • Be especially cautious with PayPal currency conversion — their 3–4% markup is one of the most expensive in the industry.
  • Ask for the "recipient amount" before confirming any transfer. A reputable service will show you exactly what lands at the destination.
