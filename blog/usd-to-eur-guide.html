USD to EUR: Everything You Need to Know

✍️ RateFlow Team 📅 January 15, 2026 ⏱ 5 min read

The US dollar to euro exchange rate — written as USD/EUR or EUR/USD — is the most actively traded currency pair in the world, accounting for roughly 20–25% of all global foreign exchange volume every single day. Whether you're a traveler planning a trip to Europe, a business paying overseas suppliers, or a freelancer receiving payments from American clients, understanding how this rate works can save you meaningful amounts of money.

At its core, the USD/EUR rate tells you how many euros one US dollar will buy. If the rate is 0.92, each dollar gets you 92 euro cents. Conversely, the EUR/USD rate — which is how forex traders typically quote it — tells you how many dollars one euro costs. These are simply two sides of the same coin.

What most people don't realize is that the rate you see on a financial news site is the mid-market rate, also called the interbank rate. This is the "real" rate that banks and large institutions use when trading with each other. The rate a retail bank or airport kiosk offers you includes a spread — a hidden markup — on top of this rate. That markup is how they make their money.

What Moves the USD/EUR Rate?

Exchange rates are set by supply and demand in the global currency market, but several key forces drive that supply and demand:

  • Interest rate decisions: When the US Federal Reserve raises interest rates, the dollar typically strengthens because higher rates attract foreign capital seeking better returns. The same logic applies when the European Central Bank (ECB) adjusts its rates. The difference in rates between the two central banks — called the interest rate differential — is one of the most powerful drivers of USD/EUR.
  • Inflation data: High inflation erodes a currency's purchasing power. If US inflation is running hotter than eurozone inflation, markets expect the Fed to keep rates higher, which can support the dollar.
  • Economic growth: A strong US economy drives demand for dollars — businesses worldwide need dollars to buy American goods, services, and financial assets. Similarly, strong European growth boosts the euro.
  • Political events: Elections, policy shifts, trade disputes, and geopolitical crises can cause sharp short-term moves. The euro tends to weaken during periods of European political uncertainty.

A Strong Dollar vs. a Weak Dollar

When the dollar strengthens — meaning one dollar buys more euros than before — it's good news for American tourists in Europe, who find their money goes further. However, it's bad news for US exporters, because American goods become more expensive for European buyers. A weak dollar has the opposite effect: European travel becomes pricier for Americans, but US exports become more competitive globally.

Over the past few years, the USD/EUR rate has seen significant swings driven by the aggressive rate-hiking cycles of both the Fed and ECB as they battled post-pandemic inflation. The dollar saw a period of notable strength in 2022, briefly reaching parity with the euro (1 USD = 1 EUR) for the first time in two decades — a milestone that dominated financial headlines. Since then, the rate has settled into more familiar territory as inflation across both economies gradually moderated.
