According to the World Bank, roughly 1.4 billion adults worldwide remain unbanked. In Sub-Saharan Africa, over 40% of the adult population lacks a bank account. Yet many of these same people regularly send or receive international money — to pay for school, support elderly parents, or receive income from abroad. Here are the real options available.

The Reality of the Unbanked Transfer Market

The traditional financial system has a clear requirement: you need a bank account to receive an international wire transfer. But "bank account" isn't the only option anymore. Mobile money has fundamentally changed what's possible, especially in Africa. And cash-based services like Western Union have always served the unbanked.

Sending Without a Bank Account (From Abroad)

If you're living abroad and don't yet have a local bank account, you can still send money home:

  • Western Union in-person (cash payment): Walk into a WU agent, pay cash, and the money can be picked up by your recipient within minutes. You need a valid ID and the recipient's details — no bank account required on your end.
  • MoneyGram (cash payment): Similar to Western Union. Available at Walmart, CVS, and other retailers in the US.
  • Prepaid debit card: Some services allow you to load a prepaid card and link it to a transfer app. This bypasses the need for a traditional bank account.

Receiving Without a Bank Account (In Africa)

  • Cash pickup (Western Union / MoneyGram): Walk to an agent, present ID and the transfer code, receive cash. Available in most African cities and many rural areas.
  • Mobile money wallet (MTN MoMo, M-Pesa, Orange Money): You only need a registered SIM card — no bank account required. Remitly and WorldRemit can deliver directly to mobile wallets.

Mobile Money: The Game Changer for the Unbanked

Mobile money has been the single biggest financial inclusion breakthrough in Africa in the past decade. Services like M-Pesa (Kenya), MTN MoMo (Cameroon, Ghana, Uganda), and Orange Money (Cameroon, Senegal, Mali) allow anyone with a registered SIM card to:

  • Receive international transfers directly to their phone
  • Pay bills, merchants, and school fees digitally
  • Save money in the wallet
  • Withdraw cash at any agent or ATM

Registering for mobile money requires only a national ID and a local SIM card — both of which the vast majority of African adults already have. For unbanked recipients, activating a mobile money wallet is a far simpler path than opening a bank account.
