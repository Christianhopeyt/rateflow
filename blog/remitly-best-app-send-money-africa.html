Africa is not a single market — it's 54 countries, dozens of currencies, vastly different banking infrastructure, and rapidly growing mobile money ecosystems. What works perfectly for a transfer to Kenya might not work at all for Cameroon. This guide cuts through the noise and tells you exactly which app to use for your specific corridor.

Why Africa Transfers Are Different

Sending money to Africa comes with unique challenges that most generic "best transfer app" guides ignore:

  • Mobile money dominance: In many African countries, more people have mobile money accounts (MTN MoMo, M-Pesa, Orange Money) than bank accounts. An app that only delivers to bank accounts will leave many recipients without access.
  • Limited banking hours and infrastructure: Bank-to-bank transfers can take days to clear, while mobile money is near-instant.
  • Exchange rate complexity: African currencies vary from freely floating (NGN, KES) to fixed pegs (XAF, XOF at 655.957/EUR) to managed floats (GHS, ETB). Each requires different handling.
  • Corridor-specific coverage: Not every service covers every country. Always verify before starting.

Wise — Best Rate, Limited African Coverage

Wise offers the best exchange rates in the industry, but their African coverage is more limited than Remitly or WorldRemit. They primarily support bank-to-bank transfers and don't currently integrate with mobile money wallets. Wise is excellent for transfers to South Africa (ZAR), Kenya (KES), Nigeria (NGN), and countries with established banking infrastructure.

WorldRemit — Strong Runner-Up

WorldRemit (now part of Zepz, same parent as Sendwave) has excellent African coverage and supports mobile money across East and West Africa. Their rates are slightly less competitive than Wise but better than most banks, and their mobile money integration is strong.

Wave — Best for West Africa

Wave charges zero fees for transfers to Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire, Mali, Burkina Faso, and a handful of other West African markets. Their rates are very competitive. The limitation is their narrow geographic coverage — they don't serve Central Africa (Cameroon, Gabon, Congo) as of 2025.
